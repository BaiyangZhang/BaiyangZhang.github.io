Consider a 1+1 dimensional Z_2 model with kink solution. Let $f(x)$ be the classical kink solution. Let $\phi$ be the field operator, $\left\lvert \Omega \right\rangle$ be the ground state in the vacuum sector, namely the vacuum itself. Then we should have $\left\langle \Omega \right\rvert \phi \left\lvert \Omega \right\rangle=0$. Now let $\left\lvert \text{kink} \right\rangle$ be the kink state that is sitting still, we should have, at the leading order $\left\langle \text{kink} \right\rvert\phi \left\lvert \text{kink} \right\rangle=f(x)$. Now introduce the displacement operator $\mathcal{D}_ {f}=\exp(-i\pi(f))$, where $\pi$ is the canonical momentum associated to $\phi(x)$ and  $\pi(f)=\int dx \, \pi(x)f(x)$ is the $\pi(x)$ smeared over $f(x)$. Then we have the following commutation relation: $\mathcal{D}_ {f}\phi D_ {f}^{\dagger}=\phi-f$. Thus if we define a new field operator $\varphi:=\mathcal{D}_ {f}\phi \mathcal{D}_ {f}^{\dagger}$, then in the kink background we have $\left\langle \text{kink} \right\rvert\varphi \left\lvert \text{kink} \right\rangle=0$. Check if the following claim is legit: In the traditional Hamiltonian perturbation theory, since the classical kink solution $f(x)$ is proportional to inverse coupling, hence in the case of weak coupling, $f(x)$ increases, renders the perturbation method invalid; but we could work with the "kink field operator" $\varphi$, since its kink expectation value is zero, just like the vacuum expectation value of $\phi$ in $\left\lvert \Omega \right\rangle$ is zero, we could use $\varphi$ to carry out the perturbation calculation in the kink background?
